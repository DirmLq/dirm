City of Islands: Railroads and Their Impact

The railroads that serve New York City played a crucial role in the development of industry and transportation. This photo essay will look at a few examples of railroads around the City, its historical use and the present use.

Prior to the construction of the East River Tunnels, which connect Sunnyside/Long Island City and the greater Long Island and New England states into the heart of New York City at Penn Station, movement between LIC and Manhattan was primarily through ferries. Trains from Long Island terminated in Long Island City where steps away, a connection to the ferry to Manhattan's East Side was easily accessible.

Although the East River Tunnels primarily served passenger traffic, freight traffic to industries in New York arrived in one of two ways: through already established rail connections to the mainland, or by barge onto gantries. Both methods still remain intact to the present day, with mainland connections available at multiple locations throughout the City and barges in Brooklyn are still active. Gantry cranes in Long Island City and a few others scattered all over the City exist as remnants of New York’s historical industrial past.

The West Side Line, with the elevated section now affectionately known as the High Line, served Manhattan’s West Side. The elevated structure was in response to freight traffic on New York’s street and the dangers they pose and the need to create its own right of way in this dense industrial area. Tenth Avenue was nicknamed Death Avenue due to collisions between the trains and the general public. Freight that was brought and produced through this line included meat and Oreos.

With the Interstate Highway Act of 1956 and under Robert Moses' control of NYC, emphasis on the road networks increased, sidelining railroads and putting them out of business. The railroad industry subsequently declined to a point where freight via railroads were non-existent for some industries and relied primarily on trucks for freight. Street level tracks were ripped up in order to provide smooth asphalt for trucks, thus decreasing the freight network in the City substantially.

Revitalization of these railroads look different at every application. While Long Island City’s barges are inactive, yet integrated into the post-industrial heritage of Gantry State Park, the High Line transformed into a jungle in its own right. Railroads, whether it be passenger or freight, will continue to impact New York’s economy for the foreseeable future.
