Maps have long been used as a tool to get a better grasp and document the world around us. It has been used to document explorations, discoveries, settlements throughout history and as we continued to understand the world, maps have been drawn and redrawn to best reflect the times. In the first half of this visual essay, we will explore a few examples of historical maps and how these maps have documented the growth of New York City and identify the artificial changes to the landscape that has been constructed throughout time.

Map makers, or cartographers, have played the important role in creating a visual imagery of geography and cities, and while these maps may be useful to understand what they are portraying, these artists also have artistic freedom in what they portray or omit, and this subjectivism is what I am exploring below in relation to New York City and its growth from its inception to the present, as well as looking at maps as a tool to document and compare New York’s growth to the changing environment within. In the second half of this visual essay, we will explore subway maps and how they represent geography as well as conceptual maps of what’s to come for the future of New York City.

Land Reclaimation


Redraft of the Castello Plan of New Amsterdam in 1660, redrawn in 1916 by John Wolcott Adams and Isaac Newton Phelps Stokes.

Source: New York Historical Society

The Castello Plan of New Amsterdam is an illustration from 1660 of Lower Manhattan during the city’s time as a Dutch colony. The 2020 Google Maps screenshot of Lower Manhattan shows the city at its present iteration. Throughout time, the area’s landmass was expanded through land reclamation to add additional space for the growing industries while retaining, more or less, the same street grid as it did back in 1660. That’s 360+ years between the Castello Plan and the present.

Map of Governors Island - 1879

Governors Island was expanded on multiple occasions in its history, notably, in the early 20th century, excavation from the building of the Lexington Avenue Subway, now the 4/5/6 lines, adding an additional 103 acres of land. The map above illustrates the size of the island in 1879 and included in the bottom left is the enlarged island in 1962 after land reclamation.

The Spuyten Duyvil Creek once wrapped around the northernmost tip of Manhattan through a long, windy route that made it an obstacle for maritime vessels to navigate easily. The Harlem River Ship Canal, which was built around 222nd Street (see 1878 map), cut through the tip of the Manhattan for an ideal, efficient route to the Hudson River. In doing so, the construction detached the Marble Hill neighborhood, thus becoming its own island for a short portion of time. Eventually, through landfill covering the old route of the Spuyten Duyvil, this piece of Manhattan is the only part of Manhattan county that is connected to the mainland.

Subway Maps

1972 Subway Map Massimo Vignelli

2017 Subway Map for the opening of the Second Avenue Subway Yoshiki Waterhouse and Beatriz Cifuentes with Massimo Vignelli

2020 Subway Map Michael Hertz Associates

Artistic freedom in the creation of specifically designed maps for a single purpose, like subway maps, create oddities that abstracts the City’s landscape and features. Here, we see the 50th Street Station of the Broadway 1 Line and the 8th Avenue C/E Lines in three different maps. The left map is Massimo Vignelli’s map of 1972 that was a radical change of designs from maps prior to it and features Broadway west of 8th Avenue, when in reality, Broadway is east of 8th Avenue. The 1972 map was renowned and well appreciated by designers, but not so much for the public because its design was not true to reality.

As the subway system expanded and as Manhattan became dense with subway lines, New York City subway maps reflected this by widening the island in order to provide more clarity, especially around Lower Manhattan. The geography, while simplified and altered, provides users of this map to understand New York’s subway in relation to New York at street level, providing a sense of navigation by using landmark and geographical cues. The center map above maps out the Subway's tracks to scale and illustrates how dense the subway lines can get.

The smaller outlying islands within New York City’s borders, like Rikers and North and South Brother Islands, tend to get forgotten in subway maps. Updates to the map would omit Rikers Island entirely, unlabel the island, or is visible and labeled, depending on what edition and year of the map you’re looking at. Parsons graduates, Acosta de la Peña, Laura Sánchez, and Misha Volf, developed and created the #SeeRikers project, which aims to identify and label Rikers Islands on subway maps throughout the subway system using a similar design language used by the MTA to bring awareness to the island’s issues and injustices.
